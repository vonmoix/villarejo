02 — Overall Impression

Gut reaction: what works, what doesn't, and the single biggest opportunity.

What works: The portfolio opens with confidence. The animated SVG paths + letter entrance creates a premium, deliberate first impression that most portfolios fail to land. The colour discipline is exceptional — the amber accent never feels decorative, always purposeful. The experience timeline is genuinely innovative and communicates leadership-at-scale better than any bullet list could.

What doesn't: The portfolio opens strongly but closes weakly. By the time a recruiter reaches the Contact section, momentum has dissipated. There is no resume download — the single highest-conversion action for any professional portfolio is absent. The section ordering places "Tech Stack" between "Skills" and "Education," interrupting what is otherwise a clean narrative arc from leadership → capability → credentials → story.

Biggest opportunity: Add a resume PDF download in the hero or contact section. A senior IT Director's CV, downloadable in one click, converts passive visitors into active candidates. Nothing else on this page has that leverage.

05 — Emotional Journey

What emotion does each section evoke? Are peak moments positive? Where do anxiety spikes occur?

Hero — Awe / Intrigue Strong peak

Letter-by-letter entrance + ambient SVG paths creates a moment of arrested attention. Rare for a portfolio. The user's emotional state at 0s is curiosity bordering on surprise — "this person spent real effort here." This is the right peak.

Experience — Respect / Trust Solid

The bifurcated timeline communicates strategic credibility. Dates, company names, and scope signals (Europe · APAC · UK) build trust incrementally. Scroll-spy dot animation maintains engagement through a long section. No anxiety valleys.

Skills / Tech Stack — Competence Neutral

Tag grids and logo rows read as proof of breadth. Emotionally neutral — functional rather than evocative. The Emerging Technologies border highlight is a good micro-signal of forward-thinking identity. Tech Stack section between Skills and Education slightly disrupts the credential arc.

Education / Certifications — Credibility Neutral

Clean table layouts. Credentialing signals are present but understated. The Technical Foundations code block at the bottom of Certifications is a nice contrast detail that elevates the section above standard.

Contact — Deflation Emotional valley

The journey ends with "Let's Connect." in a sparse layout. After the visual richness of the preceding sections, this landing feels abrupt. There is no resume download, no "what to expect if you email me," no response time signal. The peak-end rule says the last moment of an experience disproportionately shapes the overall memory. This close needs to earn the same attention the hero received.

→ Fix: Add a resume PDF download button + a one-line expectation-setter ("I respond to every message within 48 hours") to close with intention.

07 — What's Working

Three things done well, with specific reasons.

Animation Grammar

Every motion on the site follows the same enter-from-below (y: 14 → 0, opacity: 0 → 1) pattern. This creates a consistent language of arrival. The letter-by-letter hero entrance uses spring physics with tuned stiffness (130) and damping (22) — this is the right level of craft. useReducedMotion respects system accessibility settings.

Typography Discipline

Three-font system (Bricolage Grotesque display · Hanken Grotesk body · Fira Code mono) with clear role separation. Font sizes use clamp() for fluid scaling. Tracking values are intentional: tight on display (-0.027em), looser on mono (+0.12em). This is not a default stack — these choices reflect taste.

Colour Discipline

Single amber accent across the entire site. No gradient text, no multi-accent palette, no decorative colour use. OKLCH colour space gives perceptually uniform steps. The warm-grey body palette (oklch 0.49 → 0.72) creates depth without drama. Contrast ratios now pass WCAG AA across all text sizes.

Timeline Architecture

The bifurcated experience timeline is a genuinely unusual layout — left/right alternating job cards with a central scroll-spy dot. It communicates multi-threaded career progression (enterprise + consulting + product) in a way that a linear list never could. The responsive collapse to single-column on mobile is clean.

Accessibility Foundation

Skip link, ARIA mobile menu (role="dialog" aria-modal="true"), focus trap, dynamic burger label, prefers-reduced-motion respected in all Framer Motion instances. This is a stronger accessibility implementation than 90% of personal portfolios.

Information Density

Section rhythm (py-24 → py-32) gives breathing room. The background grain at 3.2% opacity adds tactile depth without visual noise. Tag grids, table layouts, and card components each carry the right density for their content type — nothing feels padded or cramped.

08 — Priority Issues

Four remaining opportunities ordered by business impact. These are post-fix findings — the 15 pre-existing issues are already resolved.

1

Missing Resume Download

P0 · Critical

What: No PDF CV download exists anywhere on the site. Why it matters: Recruiters and hiring managers expect a single-click download. The absence forces them to manually transcribe or screenshot content — a friction that causes drop-off. A portfolio without a downloadable CV is incomplete for professional circulation. Fix: Add href="/cv-manuel-villarejo.pdf" download button in both the Hero CTA area and the Contact section. Host the PDF at /public/cv-manuel-villarejo.pdf.

2

Section Ordering Interrupts Narrative Arc

P1 · High

What: Current order: Experience → Education → Skills → Tech Stack → Certifications → About → Contact. Tech Stack belongs after Skills (same capability cluster) and before Certifications. The current placement breaks the flow from "what I do" (Skills) to "what I know" (Tech Stack) to "how I prove it" (Certifications). Fix: Reorder sections in app/page.tsx: Experience → Education → Skills → Tech Stack → Certifications → About → Contact.

3

Contact Section Closes Weakly

P2 · Medium

What: "Let's Connect." is a generic heading that every portfolio uses. The section ends the experience without closure or invitation. Why it matters: Peak-end rule: the last moment of an experience shapes overall memory disproportionately. After a visually rich journey, a sparse contact section deflates the impression. Fix: (1) Replace "Let's Connect." with a more specific invitation tied to Manuel's profile — e.g. "Open to leadership conversations." (2) Add a one-line response expectation: "I reply to every message within 48h." (3) Add the resume download button here as the primary CTA above the email link.

4

No Thought-Leadership Signal for Peer Audience

P3 · Low

What: Peers and collaborators (conference organisers, fractional networks, industry peers) want to evaluate Manuel's thinking, not just his CV. There is no link to writing, talks, LinkedIn articles, or GitHub. Why it matters: For inbound opportunities from peer networks, the portfolio needs one external signal of intellectual output — even a single LinkedIn article link raises the credibility ceiling for this audience. Fix: Add an optional "Writing / Talks" link in the nav and About section if any external content exists, or add a LinkedIn articles link in the Contact section footer line.

09 — Questions to Consider

Provocative questions that might unlock better solutions.

Who is the primary audience — recruiters, CTOs, or peers — and does the information architecture reflect that priority? Recruiters need a resume download above the fold. CTOs need outcome-quantified experience entries. Peers need evidence of thinking. Right now the site serves all three equally, which means it serves none optimally.
Why a full-viewport hero with 100vh height? The letter entrance animation is compelling, but it buries immediately useful information (name, title, primary CTA) below the scroll threshold on short viewports. What if the hero were 85vh with the first section visible on entry — giving the user a taste of what follows?
The Emerging Technologies group is the most forward-looking section — "Agentic AI," "Claude Code," "AIOps" — but it's buried as the 5th of 5 skill groups. What if this were the first group? Leading with the future rather than the past changes the entire strategic positioning of the portfolio.
Experience entries describe scope but not outcomes. "Led digital transformation" is expected from an IT Director. "Led digital transformation across 14 entities, reducing infrastructure spend by 30%" is a hire. What would it look like if every experience card had one quantified outcome sentence?
